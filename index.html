Research thread / 01–04

One atmospheric story,
four connected scales.

Brown carbon is the light-absorbing fraction of organic smoke. Its climate effect depends on what is emitted, how it transforms, where it travels, and whether models capture those changes.

01 / PARTICLE

What absorbs the light?

Fire emits a changing mixture of organic compounds. I work on translating that chemistry into optical properties a global model can carry.

  • Brown carbon
  • Optics
  • Emissions

02 / PLUME

How does smoke age?

Sunlight, oxidants, temperature, and phase state reshape absorption during transport. Competing bleaching schemes turn those processes into testable hypotheses.

  • Photochemistry
  • Viscosity
  • Aircraft data

03 / TRANSPORT

Where did this air come from?

Lagrangian trajectories connect individual samples back to candidate fires, providing a physical clock for plume age and source contact.

  • LAGRANTO
  • ERA5
  • Source attribution

04 / MODEL

Does the global picture close?

GEOS-Chem links local process understanding to global aerosol burden and optical effects. Observations then reveal where that structure succeeds or fails.

  • GEOS-Chem
  • Evaluation
  • Radiative effects

About / Max Harvey

Atmospheric science across the gaps between disciplines.

My work sits where aerosol chemistry, atmospheric transport, scientific computing, and climate modelling meet.

I’m interested in what happens between a field measurement and a global conclusion: how a plume is identified, how its history is reconstructed, how uncertain mechanisms become model code, and how that model is confronted with observations.

Alongside research, I work with high-performance computing for weather and climate, including parallel programming, performance analysis, and reproducible scientific workflows.
